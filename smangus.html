The Problem

Making the invisible visible.

Smangus (司馬庫斯) is an Atayal village in Hsinchu County with a history stretching back centuries. But that history exists as scattered, static documents — colonial survey maps, migration records, field notes — disconnected from each other and inaccessible to anyone without specialist knowledge.

The challenge was both technical and human: how do you build an interface that honors the cultural weight of this material while making it genuinely explorable?

Design Thinking

Two decisions that shaped the project.

01

Temporal basemap toggle

The atlas overlays data on both a modern OSM basemap and a 1924 Japanese colonial map — switchable at any time.

A static single-layer map would flatten the temporal dimension entirely. Making it toggleable lets users literally see what changed over a century.
02

Color-coded tribal lineages

Kinaji → blue · Marikowan → red, applied consistently across all map elements and labels.

Color encoding makes spatial separation immediately readable without requiring text labels on every point — it respects the user's cognitive load.

Result

A permanent part of the archive.

Now part of Academia Sinica's permanent digital humanities archive. Three interactive layers, 100+ years of history documented, accessible through a single web experience.
