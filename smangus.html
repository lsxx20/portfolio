Design Decision 01

The map is the interface — not a decoration

Every design choice was made to serve exploration, not to illustrate. Markers are clustered at meaningful geographic scales. Labels appear only on interaction. The 1924 colonial basemap is not a background — it's evidence.

A static map of Atayal migration would show positions. An interactive map shows relationships — between time, terrain, and movement. The difference is whether the reader understands, or just sees.

Design Decision 02

Temporal depth through basemap toggle

Overlaying the same spatial data on both a modern OSM basemap and the 1924 Japanese colonial survey map lets users see how the recorded landscape has shifted. Same locations, different documentation — a century apart.

The toggle is not a UI feature. It's an argument: that the way we map a place changes what we think we know about it.

Context

What this project is about.

Smangus (司馬庫斯) is an Atayal village in Hsinchu County. Its history exists across scattered colonial survey maps, migration records, and field notes — disconnected, inaccessible to most people, and never visualized together.

This project was built during a summer internship at Academia Sinica's Geographic Information System Center. The goal was to georeference Japanese colonial-era maps, process the spatial datasets, and build an interactive atlas that makes a century of indigenous history genuinely explorable.

Result

A permanent part of the archive.

Now part of Academia Sinica's permanent digital humanities archive. Three interactive layers, over a century of spatial history, accessible through a single web experience.
